# Assignment 01: Software Development Models

This report compares six widely used software development models and explains how they differ in practice.

## Task 1: Explain Each Model
### 1. Waterfall Model (Traditional)
- **What it is:** A linear development model where teams complete one phase fully before starting the next. Typical phases are requirements, design, coding, testing, and deployment with limited backtracking.
- **When to use it:** Best for projects with stable requirements, formal approvals, and strict compliance needs.
- **One real-world example:** Large government IT procurement projects and regulated defense systems.

### 2. Agile Methodology (Iterative)
- **What it is:** An iterative way of working that delivers software in small increments and adapts quickly based on feedback. Teams prioritize collaboration, learning, and continuous improvement.
- **When to use it:** Best when requirements are likely to evolve and fast feedback from users is important.
- **One real-world example:** Spotify product teams use Agile principles to iterate on user-facing features.

### 3. Scrum Framework (Agile Framework)
- **What it is:** A structured Agile framework that organizes work into short, fixed-length sprints with defined roles and ceremonies. It focuses on delivering a potentially shippable increment each sprint.
- **When to use it:** Best for cross-functional teams building complex products that benefit from regular planning and review cycles.
- **One real-world example:** Many Microsoft software teams use Scrum sprint cycles for product delivery.

### 4. Kanban (Visual Workflow)
- **What it is:** A visual flow-based method that tracks work items on a board and limits work in progress. It improves throughput by making bottlenecks visible.
- **When to use it:** Best for continuous delivery environments, support teams, and projects with a steady stream of incoming tasks.
- **One real-world example:** Atlassian teams commonly use Kanban boards to manage support and maintenance work.

### 5. DevOps (Culture + Practice)
- **What it is:** A culture and set of engineering practices that unite development and operations for faster, safer software delivery. It relies on automation, CI/CD pipelines, monitoring, and shared responsibility.
- **When to use it:** Best for products that require frequent releases, reliable operations, and rapid incident response.
- **One real-world example:** Netflix uses DevOps and automation to deploy and operate services at scale.

### 6. Spiral Model (Risk-Driven)
- **What it is:** An iterative model organized into loops where each cycle emphasizes risk identification, prototyping, and evaluation before moving forward.
- **When to use it:** Best for high-risk, high-cost, or technically uncertain projects where early risk control is critical.
- **One real-world example:** Aerospace and defense R&D programs often use spiral-style risk-driven planning.
